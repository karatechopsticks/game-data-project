Day One: Garry's Incident is a 2013 PC survival game developed and published by Wild Games Studio.[2] Initially released on 25 September 2013, Day One: Garry's Incident follows Garry Friedman, a pilot who recently lost his wife and daughter, as he is transported to the Amazon Rainforest following the explosion of the Yellowstone Caldera by magical ancient artifacts. He encounters rabid wildlife that is later found to be linked to a ritual by the natives.

Since release, Day One: Garry's Incident has received overwhelmingly negative reviews from audiences and critics, as well as generating considerable controversy due to copyright acts and censorship on the website YouTube as well as a number of fake reviews posted on the review aggregate site Metacritic.[3]

Plot[edit]

Day One: Garry's Incident follows Garry Friedman. Garry's wife and daughter were recently killed in an accident of some kind, leaving the middle-aged Garry depressed. He has since drowned his sorrows in alcohol and become a pilot, willing to do any job, no matter how dangerous, for the money. On Friday, 13 June 2014, Garry collects some cargo in his plane from a research facility in Yellowstone National Park, en route to Nevada, with the drop location updated along the way. However, as he is leaving, the Yellowstone Caldera begins to erupt, and the plane begins spiralling out of control. This triggers the artefacts he is carrying in the back of the plane, which transport him to the Amazon Rainforest. As the plane descends, Garry passes out of consciousness, the trees breaking his fall and keeping him alive. In the jungle, Garry discovers an ancient native civilization, finds food, builds his own weapons and survives, discovering artefacts left by aliens hundreds of years previously.[4]

Controversy[edit]

YouTube controversy[edit]

On 18 October 2013, Wild Games Studio made a copyright claim against YouTube critic TotalBiscuit in an attempt to remove his video review of the game, titled "WTF Is... Day One Garry's Incident?",[5] which was heavily critical of the game. Their copyright claim was briefly successful and the video was removed from YouTube, before being later reinstated.

Wild Games' response that they were protecting their copyright "because Total Biscuit has no right to make advertising revenues with our license" was rebuked by Steam users and professional reviewers alike, who pointed out that nearly all game review websites and videos are funded by advertising, many mainstream professional game review websites were also highly critical of this response, with one stating "it’s implicit that some kind of monetization process is set up for professional or even semi-professional sites and channels".[6] No other reviews (written, spoken or video), and residing on monetized websites, were known to be issued with similar copyright takedown notices by the company.[7] Kotaku stated that other YouTube videos "(...) some even containing equally negative commentary - were never removed from YouTube, nor was our own video look at the game".[8] It has also been stated that using copyright material for criticism is protected by the fair use doctrine.[9]

In response to the incident, TotalBiscuit issued a response on Twitter, in which he stated "Long story short. Dev sends code, code used to make critique, dev dislikes critique, dev abuses system to censor critique.".[9] He later stated "The idea that you could use copyright as a spear to attack those who are criticizing you is an affront to free speech and freedom of the press (...) It's horrendously anti-consumer, it's unquestionably censorship".[10]

On 20 October 2013, TotalBiscuit made a video on the issue,[11] reaching 2 million views in 3 days. Techdirt reported that the new TotalBiscuit video revealed other "shady" practices by Wild Games Studio including "(badly) astroturfing reviews, and accusations that they tried to pump up their Kickstarter campaign (for the same game) by (1) having the CEO of the company pledge $10,000 upfront, and (2) having others pledge high amounts and withdraw them later (suggesting that it was just to make it look like there was momentum for the campaign)".[12] The proceeds of the video were said to be donated to Electronic Frontier Foundation.[citation needed]

Fake Metacritic User Review Accusations[edit]

Employees of the company were accused of posting false user reviews on the MetaCritic website to increase the average score by a Steam user (on a post which has since been deleted), however, a game review website[13] reported on the controversy when the thread on the Steam forums was still live, the site states that "all of the accounts used to post these supposedly bogus viewpoints were recently created and have never posted on anything else".[13] The studio denied involvement and issued the following response: "Wild Games Studio has not encouraged employees to write reviews nor did we hear of someone in the company doing that. However, I have received a message of one of the reviewers telling me he wanted to help and gave us a good review. We do not want people writing reviews that do not express their opinion only in the purpose to help as it is not representative of their experience. After taking a look at some of the reviews mentioned, they look so similar that I believe that they might have been planted by someone to try to call for a rise to lower scores. We encourage you not to write reviews to "help" if it does not represent your opinion".[13] The accusation that the positive reviews were made by Wild Games Studio were subsequently included in TotalBiscuit's response video.[12]

Steam Forum Censorship[edit]

Wild Games Studio initially responded on the game's dedicated Steam forum to defend their actions, this post has since been removed.[14] An apology posted on Steam by the developers has also been removed.

As gaming (and even mainstream news)[15] websites picked up on the story, many Steam users flocked to the game's forum to show their disapproval. In October 2013, the website Gaming Blend reported that "The incident reached a fever-pitch due to all the complaints on the Steam forum where people are bemoaning the poor quality of the game, which released back on September 25th. This is in addition to other critics railing on the title as well";[16] these posts have been deleted (there is a moderated "Bug and Fix Report" pinned post).[17]

Original posts by Steam users in the biggest threads which referenced the YouTube video removal, including responses and posts made by the developers themselves as well as subsequent posts, responses, arguments and all negative comments regarding the game have been deleted from the Steam message boards by the studio, further inflaming users. The forum has since been locked down, and displays the message "Posting privileges: Only players who own Day One: Garry's Incident are allowed to post in this forum", critical posts continue to be deleted or locked. However, the developers insist they only delete comments which are "aggressive, insulting or disrespectful".[18]

A post on the Steam forum entitled "review coverup", which many users had contributed to and the game developers had replied to, has now also been deleted[19] A huge number of posts were made after the "TotalBiscuit" incident, of those that survive many have been locked, deleted or the Steam users posting on them have been locked out by the forum lockdown (to game purchasers only).

Response to Controversies[edit]

On 21 October 2013, Wild Games Studio apologized for the controversy and revoked the copyright complaint.

The controversy saw a huge backlash from gamers, gaming websites, and commentators alike, with many accusing the company of a deliberate attempt to stifle bad publicity before the game's launch by abusing the copyright system (reporting on the incident gaming website Destructoid stated "Copyright law is supposed to exist for the purposes of defense, not attack. I have nothing but incalculable contempt for those who wield it as the latter"[10]), censorship[9] and suppressing free speech.[20] The coverage of the studio's attempt to cover up the bad review was described as an example of the Streisand effect.[12]

By November 2013, over 1400 reviewers had submitted their user scores to the Metacritic website, where the game had a score of 0.5/10.[3] Many of the negative reviews on Metacritic were in response to the accusations that Wild Games Studio employees had created fake accounts to review the game on Metacritic.[13] Whilst other games have suffered from a consumer backlash on the user review section of Metacritic,[21] this led to Day One: Garry's Incident receiving the lowest Metacritic score of any PC game in the site's history.[22]
